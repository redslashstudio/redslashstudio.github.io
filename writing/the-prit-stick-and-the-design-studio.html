From Pritt Stick to Design Studio

I've always had a passion for branding. When I was sixteen, I had dreams of founding my own company — what I'd best describe now as a cottage-industry version of the Virgin Group. It'd start with clothing, then music, then who knows — all built on an independent ethos. I decided to call it Small World Company.

I wrote a one-page mission statement — a manifesto, if you will — then set about coming up with a look for it.

My drawing skills were basic, so I grabbed some Microsoft Clip Art, distorted and stretched it, and Pritt-Sticked it to the top of a letterhead. I took it down to the local printers and, using money from my weekend job at the motorway services, got it produced onto some fancy, high-GSM paper.

My first piece of brand design. And honestly? I was pretty pleased with it. But I didn't quite know what to actually do with Small World Company, so it went in the drawer, and I went off to university.

You see, I'm not a designer; not in the Photoshop sense of the word. And for most of my career, that didn't really matter. Working in broadcast — launching shows, building brands at the BBC and Channel 4 — I was surrounded by designers, developers and trailer producers who could handle that side of things. I might know what I wanted something to look or feel like, but I could rely on others to bring it to life.

Then I left — at the end of 2020 — and the gap came back. I had all the same instincts. I could still look at a website or a brand and tell you exactly what wasn't working. But I couldn't do anything about it on my own. I had visions for things I wanted to build: a clothing brand, an initiative supporting grassroots music venues, all those old childhood ideas resurfacing. And every time, the same problem. I could see what needed to exist, but I couldn't make it happen.

I was basically back to being the kid with the Pritt Stick. Just older, and considerably more frustrated about it. And that's just how things stayed for a while.

Then, in November last year, something magical happened.

I was asked to help develop a brand for Steadman, the AI training and transformation company I often collaborate with. My natural instinct was: right, I need a designer, I need a web developer. But I was told to practise what we preached — to work out how to use AI to do it myself.

So I started playing around. The first few iterations were pretty rough, and there were moments when I wanted to give up. But I kept going, and gradually something clicked — a point where it felt like me and the AI actually understood what we were looking for together.

Using Google Gemini's image generator, we mocked up what the homepage hero might look like. And alongside it, the AI produced some HTML — trying to show me what a button could look like. Not being a coder, I glossed over it. What was I supposed to do with HTML?

But a few hours later, curiosity got the better of me. I went back and asked the AI to walk me through what to do with the HTML.

"OMG that is lush!"

I asked it to change a colour, and it changed. I asked it to move something, and it moved. I asked for a button with a shadow that disappeared on hover, and it built it. I kept tweaking, kept asking, and every single time — it just worked.

Within a week of asking what CSS was, I'd shipped an entire website — responsive, interactive, the lot.

I hadn't learned to code. I still can't code. But twenty years of knowing what good looks like suddenly had somewhere to go.

Over the following months, the capability compounded. I built a speculative website for a podcast festival. Then one for a healthcare startup. Then the one you're looking at right now. Each one more sophisticated than the last, each one building on what I'd figured out from the one before.

Not only that, but it dawned on me that the code wasn't just building websites — it was encoding the brand. The HTML was like DNA. The same system that built the site could produce LinkedIn graphics, pitch decks, social content. Everything belonging to the same visual language, all pulling from the same source.

And then a thought: what if I could encode my own way of thinking about brand and design into a system for other people to use? The instincts I'd spent twenty years developing — the audience brain, the brand brain, the creative brain — what if I could make them accessible to others?

Somewhere in the middle of all that, Red Slash Studio happened. Not because I planned it — because I realised that the thing I'd been trying to do since I was sixteen was suddenly, actually possible.

Same impulse as the Pritt Stick letterhead, really. Just twenty-odd years and a lot of photocopier toner apart.
